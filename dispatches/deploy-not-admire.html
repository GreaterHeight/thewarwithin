War Letter Friday, 28 February 2026 Theatre VI · Leadership & Stewardship

Everything You Have Been Given Was Given to Be Deployed, Not Admired

The difference between a talent and a tool is deployment. A talent sits inside the person. A tool changes something in the world. The moment you decide to deploy what you have been given is the moment your assignment becomes operational.

A master leaves. Three servants are given resources proportional to their capacity. Two deploy what they were given. The third buries his. When the master returns, it is not the amount that is evaluated — it is the decision. The one who buried his talent did not steal it. He kept it safe, preserved it carefully, and returned it intact. He was, by most reasonable measures, responsible.

He was called wicked and lazy.

This is the most disturbing verdict in the parable. Keeping what you were given without deploying it is not safety — it is failure. Preservation without deployment is, in the economy of assignment, the same as loss.

“The buried talent was not stolen. It was not squandered. It was simply not used. And that was enough.”

I meet people regularly who are admiring their gifts. They know they have them. They protect them carefully — from criticism, from the risk of failure, from the exposure that deployment requires. They are waiting for the conditions to be perfect. They are waiting for the confidence to arrive. They are waiting for the fear to subside.

The fear does not subside before deployment. It subsides during it. The confidence does not arrive before you act. It accumulates as the result of acting. Everything you have been given was given to be used.

Stewardship is not conservation. The full doctrine: you have been entrusted with something that belongs to another, to be deployed in the world in ways that produce return beyond what you received. The keeping is only legitimate insofar as it serves the operating.

The question stewardship actually asks is not “am I keeping this safe?” The question is: “What am I producing with what I have been given?” If the answer is nothing — if the gift is being preserved in perfect safety, generating no return, producing no change in the world — then the stewardship has already failed.

The talent in the ground was perfectly safe. It was also perfectly useless. The parable does not reward safety. It rewards deployment.
